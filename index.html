Sri Banashankari
Amma Temple

Banashankari · Bengaluru · Karnataka

A sacred shrine dedicated to Goddess Banashankari — the sixth incarnation of Durga — nestled in South Bengaluru. Established in 1915 and rooted in a tradition over 1,400 years old, this is Bengaluru's most revered and richest temple.

Divine Origins

History & Spiritual Significance

📜

Ancient Roots

The original Banashankari shrine traces back to the 7th century AD during the Badami Chalukya dynasty. King Jagadekamalla I installed the goddess image in 603 AD. The Skanda Purana and Padma Purana describe how Goddess Shakambari — Banashankari's earlier form — slew the demon Durgamasura to protect humanity.

🏛️

Founding of Bengaluru Temple

In 1915, a devoted merchant named Basappa (Somanna) Shetty dreamt of the Goddess commanding him to bring her idol from Badami. He obeyed, and the idol was ceremonially installed on May 21, 1915 — giving the entire neighbourhood its name: Banashankari.

🦁

The Deity

The black stone idol depicts Goddess Banashankari seated majestically on a lion, trampling the demon under her foot. She holds eight arms bearing a trishul (trident), damaru (drum), khadga (sword), khetha (shield), skull cup, Vedic scriptures, a ghanta (war bell), and a severed demon head — embodying total divine power.

🕯️

The Rahukala Miracle

The temple's most unique feature: the Goddess is worshipped during Rahukala — traditionally the most inauspicious time in Hindu belief. Devotees strongly believe that praying here during Rahukala removes all hardships, poverty, and suffering. This practice stems from a miraculous legend of three divine women who appeared during Rahukala and disappeared after the puja, believed to be the Goddess herself.

🍋

Lemon Lamp Offering

The signature ritual of this temple is the Nimb Deepa — lighting oil lamps inside half-cut lemon peels with the pulp removed. This is offered by thousands of devotees as a symbol of surrendering one's ego and illuminating the soul with divine light. The lemon lamp stand (Nimb Deepa Stambha) near the main temple holds these sacred offerings.

🌿

Shakambari – The Vegetarian Goddess

Banashankari is also called Shakambari — meaning "The Goddess of Vegetables." She is worshipped with offerings of fresh fruits, vegetables, coconuts, and flowers rather than animal sacrifice, making this temple beloved across communities for its peaceful and vegetarian devotional traditions.

Ritual Practices

  • 🪔Abhishekam — Ritual bathing of the idol with milk, honey, water, and sacred substances. Purifies the devotee's mind and body.
  • 🌸Alankaram — Decoration of the Goddess with fresh flowers, silk garments, and jewellery. Offered by families as a mark of devotion.
  • 📿Archana — Recitation of the Goddess's 108 names (Astothram) while offering flowers one by one at the feet of the deity.
  • 🔥Deeparadhana — Ritual of waving lit oil lamps before the Goddess. Symbolises the removal of ignorance and the arrival of divine wisdom.
  • 🍋Nimb Deepa (Lemon Lamp) — The temple's signature offering — a lamp lit inside a halved lemon, representing surrender of ego to the Divine Mother.
Sacred Calendar

Festivals & Celebrations

January · February

Banashankari Jatre

The grand annual fair — the most spectacular celebration at the temple, drawing hundreds of thousands of devotees. Features cultural programmes, a chariot procession (Rath Yatra), boat festival, and market fair.

March · April (Chaitra)

Chaitra Navaratri

Nine nights of worship of Navadurga — the nine forms of the Divine Mother. Each day is dedicated to a different form of the Goddess with special pujas.

September 13

Banashankari Jayanti

Birthday of Goddess Banashankari Amma, celebrated with great pomp. Thousands of devotees stream in for special abhishekam, alankaram, and midnight puja.

September · October (Ashwin)

Navaratri / Dussehra

Nine nights of the warrior Goddess. Culminates on Vijayadasami with Ayudha Puja — the blessing of tools and instruments — a deeply Kannadiga tradition.

October · November

Deepavali

The festival of lights celebrated with special oil lamp offerings and illumination of the entire temple complex. Thousands of lemon lamps are lit by devotees.

February · March

Maha Shivratri

A grand fair is organised at the temple in honour of Lord Shiva, the consort of Parvati (of whom Banashankari is an incarnation). All-night vigil and puja.

December · January (Pushya)

Temple Anniversary

The Brahma Rathotsava — the grand chariot festival marking the founding anniversary of the temple. The Utsava Murthy (festival idol) is carried in a decorated chariot around the temple.

Practical Information

Visitor Guide

Do's & Customs

  • 👗Dress modestly in traditional attire — sarees, salwars for women; dhotis, kurtas or formal clothes for men. Avoid shorts or sleeveless clothing.
  • 👟Remove footwear before entering the temple compound. Footwear stands are available outside.
  • 🙏Maintain silence and reverence during puja times. Switch phones to silent mode.
  • 🕯️You may purchase lemon lamps (Nimb Deepa) at the counter and offer them — it is the most auspicious offering at this temple.
  • 🌺Offerings of fresh coconuts, flowers, fruits, and vegetables are considered auspicious for Shakambari Devi.
  • 📷Photography in the outer compound is generally permitted but not inside the sanctum. Always ask the priests.

Important Notes

  • ⏰Arrive early (before 8 AM) on Tuesdays, Fridays, and Sundays to avoid very long queues.
  • 🔔During Rahukala — which changes daily — the temple atmosphere is especially intense and spiritually charged. Devotees specifically plan visits around this time.
  • 🏛️The temple follows Shaiva Agama Siddhantha rites strictly. All rituals are conducted by trained hereditary priests.
  • 🌍The temple is open to all — devotees of any faith are welcome to visit and experience the peace of the temple.
  • 💰Sevas (special pujas) can be booked at the temple counter. Prasad is distributed after each major puja.
  • 🌧️Best time to visit: Early mornings (6–8 AM) on weekdays for a peaceful darshan. During festivals, expect large, joyful crowds.
Getting There

How to Reach

📍 Sri Banashankari Temple, S. Kariyappa Road, Banashankari Temple Ward, Bengaluru – 560070

🚇

Metro

Banashankari Metro Station on the Green Line (Namma Metro). Just 5–10 minutes walk from the station to the temple.

🚌

Bus (BMTC)

Numerous BMTC buses stop at Banashankari Bus Stop, directly outside the temple complex on Kanakapura Road.

🛺

Auto / Cab

Readily available throughout Bengaluru. Ola, Uber, and local autos all have Banashankari Temple as a common destination.

🚗

By Car

Ample parking available near the temple. 8.5 km from Bengaluru City Railway Station via Kanakapura Road.

✈️

From Airport

Kempegowda International Airport is ~40 km away. Take the Purple Line Metro or a cab to reach the temple.
